What is an IUD (intrauterine device) Also called the copper coil

  • An IUD (intrauterine device), also called a copper coil, is a small plastic T-shape that a doctor or nurse puts into your womb (uterus). It stops pregnancy by releasing copper into your womb.
  • This page is about the copper coil. For information about the hormonal coil, also called Mirena coil, see intrauterine system (IUS).
  • An IUD is over 99% effective. This means that less than 1 in 100 women with an IUD will get pregnant in one year.
  • An IUD can also be used as a form of emergency contraception.
  • An IUD works as soon as it's put in and lasts for 5 or 10 years, depending on the type.
  • You can usually get an IUD after having a baby, and it is safe to use while breastfeeding.
  • You can get pregnant straight away after an IUD is removed.
Information:

An IUD does not stop you getting sexually transmitted infections (STIs). Use a condom every time you have sex to protect you and your partner against STIs.
