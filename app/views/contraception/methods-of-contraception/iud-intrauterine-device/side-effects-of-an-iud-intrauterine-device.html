Side effects of an IUD (intrauterine device) Also called the copper coil

Side effects of an IUD

An IUD (intrauterine device), also called a copper coil, is a non-hormonal form of contraception. This means there are no hormonal side effects, such as acne, headaches or breast tenderness.

After you get an IUD fitted:

  • you might have some pain and bleeding for a few days
  • your periods may become heavier, longer or more painful – this may improve after a few months
  • you have a small chance of getting thrush that keeps coming back – speak to your GP if this happens

Your partner should not be able to feel your IUD during sex. If they can, see a doctor or nurse for a check-up.

How well does an IUD work

An IUD is over 99% effective at preventing pregnancy.

This means that less than 1 in 100 women using an IUD as their method of contraception get pregnant each year.

Risks of an IUD

An IUD is very safe, but there are some rare risks, including:

  • pelvic infections, usually within 3 weeks of having an IUD fitted
  • your IUD moving or being pushed out by the womb, usually within 3 months of having an IUD fitted
  • damage to the womb
  • ectopic pregnancy in the rare case that your IUD fails

Urgent advice: Ask for an urgent GP appointment or get help from NHS 111 if:

You've had an IUD fitted and:

  • you have pain or tenderness in your lower abdomen
  • you have a high temperature
  • you have abnormal or smelly discharge
  • you have very heavy vaginal bleeding
  • you think you might be pregnant
  • you cannot feel the threads of your IUD, or they feel shorter or longer than usual

You can call 111 or get help from 111 online.

If you cannot feel your IUD threads

If you cannot feel the threads when you check for them you might not be protected against pregnancy, and you may need emergency contraception.

Use another form of contraception until a doctor or nurse has checked your IUD.
