How to take the combined pill

Taking different types of combined pill

There are 2 main types of combined pill (also called the pill).

Everyday (ED) pill

You take the everyday (ED) pill every day.

It comes with 7 dummy pills which do not contain any hormones. These are a different colour to the active pills.

21-day pill

You take this for 21 days then have a break for 7 days.

Sometimes a doctor may recommend taking the 21-day pill with a shorter break. This is called a tailored regime.

Information:

For more information, check the leaflet that comes with your pills, or speak to a doctor, nurse or pharmacist.

Starting the combined pill

If you're taking the pill for the first time, you can normally start taking it at any point in your menstrual cycle. The advice may be different if you have a short menstrual cycle (less than 24 days).

Speak to a doctor or nurse if you're not sure when to start taking the pill.

Starting on day 1 to 5 of your cycle

If you start taking the pill in the first 5 days of your cycle (day 1 is the first day of your period), you will be protected from pregnancy straight away.

You will not need to use another type of contraception.

Starting after day 5 of your cycle

If you start taking the pill after day 5 of your cycle (more than 5 days after the start of your period), you will not be protected from pregnancy straight away.

It is possible to get pregnant from day 5 of your cycle, so you will need to use another type of contraception, such as condoms. Keep using them until you have taken the pill for 7 days.

Important: Important

If there's a risk you could already be pregnant when you start taking the pill, take a pregnancy test 3 weeks after the last time you had unprotected sex.


How well does the combined pill work?

You need to take the combined pill at the same time every day for it to work properly.

When used correctly, the pill is more than 99% effective. This means it stops 99 in 100 women getting pregnant each year.

If not used correctly, it is 91% effective and stops 91 in 100 women getting pregnant. This is because mistakes can happen in real life. You might forget to take your pills, or take them at the wrong time of day.


How to change to a different pill

If you want advice about changing your contraceptive pill, talk to a GP, contraceptive nurse (sometimes called a family planning nurse), or go to a sexual health clinic.

When changing to a new pill, you will usually need to start the new pill immediately, or wait until the day after you take the last of your old pills.

The doctor or nurse may tell you to use another type of contraception such as condoms during the changeover. The new pill may take a short time to start working.
