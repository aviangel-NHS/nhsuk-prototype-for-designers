Side effects of the combined pill

Common side effects of the combined pill

You may have some side effects when you first start taking the combined contraceptive pill (also called the pill), but they usually go away after a few weeks.

Common side effects include:

  • headache
  • feeling sick (nausea)
  • tender or sore breasts
  • mood swings

If youâ€™re still bothered by side effects after a few months, it may help to change to a different pill.

Breakthrough bleeding (bleeding between periods) is also common in the first few months.

Taking the pill may give you high blood pressure.

Risks of the combined pill

There are some other problems linked to taking the combined pill. However, the chance of getting these is small.

Blood clots

The risk of getting a blood clot is very small and affects less than 1 in 1,000 people using combined hormonal contraception.

Your doctor will check if you have certain risk factors before prescribing the pill.

There is a potential risk of:

  • a blood clot in your leg or your lung
  • stroke
  • heart attack

Cancer

Taking the pill can slightly increase your risk of developing:

  • breast cancer
  • cervical cancer

However, 10 years after you stop taking the pill, your risk of breast cancer and cervical cancer goes back to normal.

Taking the pill can also lower your risk of developing:

  • womb (uterus) cancer
  • ovarian cancer
  • bowel cancer
