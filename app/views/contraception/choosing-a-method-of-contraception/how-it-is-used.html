How it is used

Issues you may have around using different methods of contraception may include:

  • how often do you need to use it?
  • can you use it yourself at home or will you need to see a doctor or nurse?
  • what impact will it have on your sex life?

How often it is used

You need to use some methods of contraception every time you have sex. Others involve taking a pill every day, or changing a patch or vaginal ring every week. Some methods, called long-acting methods, can last for several months or years.

Every time you have sex

Methods are:

  • condoms
  • diaphragm
  • cap

Every day or most days

Methods are:

  • combined pill (the pill)
  • progestogen-only pill (the mini pill)

Every week

Methods are:

  • contraceptive patch
  • vaginal ring

Long-acting methods

Methods are:

  • IUD (copper coil), which lasts for 5 or 10 years depending on the type
  • IUS (Mirena coil), which lasts for 3 or 5 years depending on the type
  • contraceptive implant, which lasts for 3 years
  • contraceptive injection, which lasts for 8 or 13 weeks depending on the type

Permanent methods

Methods are:

  • vasectomy
  • female sterilisation

How you use it

You can use, or take, some methods of contraception yourself. With others, you need to see a doctor or nurse to give it to you.

Methods you can use yourself

Methods are:

  • combined pill
  • progestogen-only pill
  • contraceptive patch
  • condoms
  • diaphragm
  • cap
  • vaginal ring

Methods that a doctor or nurse give you

Methods are:

  • IUD (copper coil)
  • IUS (Mirena coil)
  • contraceptive implant
  • contraceptive injection

Contraception and sex

Some methods need to be used every time you have sex, while others you don't have to think about before having sex.

You need to put a condom in place immediately before you have vaginal sex. You can put a diaphragm or cap in a few hours before having sex.

You do not need to think about other methods of contraception every time you have sex. This applies to contraceptive pills and patches, vaginal rings, contraceptive implants and injections, and IUD and IUS coils.

If a partner can feel your IUD or IUS during sex it could mean your coil has moved out of a place. Contact a GP or sexual health clinic for advice.

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs)

Only condoms can protect you against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

STIs are not only spread through penetrative sex (penis in vagina sex). Some infections can be spread during oral sex, anal sex and skin to skin contact.

Side effects and sex

Some methods of contraception can cause side effects like headaches or mood swings for a few months after you start them.

If you are concerned that your method of contraception is causing problems with your sex life, then talk to a GP or a doctor or nurse at a sexual health clinic. They may be able to recommend a different method of contraception for you.
